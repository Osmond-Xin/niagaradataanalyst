Component 02 — Species Vulnerability Profile

Which Trees Are Most Threatened? Ash & Norway Maple First.

Ash (Fraxinus) — VULN = 5

11,222 trees city-wide face near-certain loss from the Emerald Ash Borer (EAB): documented 99% mortality within 8–10 years of infestation (CFIA 2021).

Ward 13 epicentre: 2,232 Ash trees — the highest concentration in Hamilton and the primary EAB intervention target.

Action: Treat or remove all Ash before 2028 — starting Ward 13.

Norway Maple — VULN = 4

32,391 trees city-wide — the most numerous high-vulnerability species. VULN score of 4 assigned per Hamilton Urban Forest Strategy Technical Report (p.15): non-native, shallow-rooted, high storm damage risk, ecological displacement. Independently confirmed as invasive in Ontario by the Ontario Invasive Plant Council (OIPC).

Action: Replace Norway Maple with native species on a rolling 10-year schedule.

Drought Stress Index Heatmap (Comp 03)

Heat–rain imbalance drives tree stress

DSI = TXx_delta / PRCPTOT_delta_pct (normalised 0–1 across 90 combinations). By 2080: SSP5-8.5 DSI = 0.81 vs SSP2-4.5 = 0.41 — a 40-point gap. Baseline: ECCC Climate Normals 1981–2010, Hamilton RBG/Mount Hope station.

Counter-intuitive finding: Under SSP2-4.5, drought stress is higher at 2030 than 2050. Mid-century precipitation gains outpace temperature rise, temporarily reducing the heat–rain imbalance before long-term warming resumes.

Data source: ECCC CanDCS-M6 — CMIP6 Statistical Downscaling for Canada (climate-scenarios.canada.ca, 2023). Baseline climate: ECCC Climate Normals 1981–2010, Hamilton RBG / Mount Hope station (climate.weather.gc.ca). All 15 wards share the same DSI within each scenario/horizon cell (10 km grid).

Component 05 — Ward 10 Action Roadmap

Ward 10: Hamilton’s Highest-Risk Ward — Concrete Intervention Timeline

Why Ward 10 first?

Ward 10 has the highest V_SCORE in Hamilton: 5.39 (SSP5-8.5, 2080). It hosts 17,195 trees delivering $512,411/yr in ecosystem services — 100% of which is at risk under high-emission projections.

Model validation: Spearman ρ(ward V_SCORE, ward mean P_DECLINE) = 0.482 (SSP5-8.5, 2080) — above 0.35 threshold, confirming internal model consistency.

Three-timeline intervention

Now–2028: Remove all Ash and Norway Maple (highest VULN species first).
2028–2035: Replant with native species — Eastern White Cedar, Red Oak, Sugar Maple (target: 4,000 trees across 7 wards, $2M CR-UFIM budget).
Long-term: Grow total canopy beyond current baseline through ongoing monitoring, maintenance, and community forestry programs.

Note: comp_01 shows Ward 10 in the city-wide financial context (why Ward 10 matters). This component shows the internal Ward 10 roadmap (what to do, and when).

Three Actions That Must Start Now

01

Start in Ward 13 — No Barriers, Act Now

3,395 high-risk trees on public land. Ward 13 has the highest Ash concentration (2,232 trees) and 68% public ownership — the City can begin EAB removal and native replanting immediately, without private-property negotiation.

02

Remove All Ash by 2028

EAB kills 99% of Ash trees within 8–10 years of infestation (CFIA 2021). Hamilton’s 11,222 Ash trees (VULN=5) are already effectively lost without emergency intervention. Ward 13 first, then all other wards.

03

Invest $2M with CR-UFIM Split

40% species diversification · 35% canopy expansion · 25% monitoring. Target: 4,000 native trees across 7 wards, all on public land. This single investment protects $1.39M in annual ecosystem services at risk.
