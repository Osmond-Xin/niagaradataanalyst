Hamilton's Urban Tree Inventory

Hamilton maintains 275,156 city-owned trees across 15 wards — one of Ontario's largest municipal urban forest datasets. The raw data contained 281,744 records, of which 6,588 were removed due to non-existing status or unrealistic measurements (e.g., DBH values above 500 cm).

The cleaned inventory spans 314 unique tree species with complete ward assignments. Norway Maple, Sugar Maple, and Honey Locust account for the most abundant species by count.

Why this matters: A high-quality, deduplicated inventory is the foundation for every downstream analysis. Errors in species names or ward assignments would produce misleading vulnerability scores.

Figure 1 — Top species by count. Norway Maple dominates Hamilton's urban forest, already flagging a monoculture concern addressed in Step 3.

Figure 2 — Tree diameter (DBH) distribution. Most trees are young with small trunk sizes, indicating limited adaptive capacity in the current forest.

Figure 3 — Tree count per ward. Ward 1 has the largest tree population (24,141); Ward 2 the smallest (9,917).

Hamilton's Climate Record (1866 – 2026)

Climate data was assembled from three consecutive weather stations covering 160 years of observations: HAMILTON (1866–1958), HAMILTON RBG (1953–~2000), and HAMILTON RBG CS (1994–2026). Station records were deduplicated and combined into a single seamless monthly dataset of 1,641 observations.

A data gap exists between 1888–1896 due to station closure and is clearly marked in all trend analyses. Annual summaries include only complete years with all 12 months present.

Hamilton is warming. The 10-year rolling average shows a clear upward trend, particularly accelerating after 1980. This long-term warming directly increases heat stress on the urban forest.

Figure 4 — Hamilton annual mean temperature (1866–2025) with 10-year rolling average. The warming trend is unmistakable, especially post-1980.

Figure 5 — Raw annual mean temperature. Year-to-year variability is high but the long-term direction is upward.

Figure 6 — Annual total precipitation. Variability is high with no strong directional trend, but extreme events are increasing.

Species Climate Resilience Scoring

Each of the 306 species in the Metro Vancouver Urban Forest Climate Adaptation database was scored across four climate stress dimensions: extreme heat (AHS heat zone), hydroclimate stress (drought + saturated soil tolerance), pollution tolerance, and future climate suitability. Scores were normalized 0–1 and averaged into a single resilience score.

Most species are only moderately resilient. The mean resilience score is 0.43, and the majority of species fall between 0.25–0.50. Only a small group achieves scores above 0.75, making species selection for replanting critically important.

Figure 7 — Distribution of species resilience scores. The bell curve peaks around 0.40–0.45, with few species in the "Very High" range above 0.75.

Figure 8 — Top 15 most climate-resilient species. Common Hackberry (0.938) and Asian Pear (0.917) lead the list.

Land Use & Built Environment by Ward

Ward-level land use data (2022) was cleaned to compute each ward's built share (residential, commercial, industrial, transport) and green share (parks and open space). These metrics directly feed into the heat index and vulnerability calculations in Step 4.

Ward 3 is the most urbanised. With 81.7% built coverage and only 5.8% green space, it faces the highest urban heat island effect — a critical driver of tree vulnerability. Ward 13 is the only ward where green space exceeds built area.

Figure 9 — Built share vs. green share per ward. Wards 3, 7, and 8 are the most urbanised; Ward 13 is the most vegetated.

Figure 10 — Scatter plot of built vs. green share. Built-heavy wards cluster bottom-right; greener wards top-left.

Road Network & Planting Corridors

Hamilton's road network spans 3,835 km across 20,311 segments. Roads were classified and joined to ward boundaries using spatial intersection. Local residential streets account for 1,899 km (~49.5%) of the total — the primary corridor for future urban tree planting.

Arterial roads impose the highest stress on street trees (traffic pollution, heat from asphalt, salt damage), and their share per ward feeds directly into the road stress score used in Step 5.

Figure 11 — Total road length by classification. Local streets dominate at 1,899 km, offering the most potential for new tree planting.

Figure 12 — Road class composition by ward. Ward 3 has the highest proportion of arterial roads, intensifying street-tree stress.
