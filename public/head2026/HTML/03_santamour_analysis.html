The 10/20/30 Diversity Rule Explained

The Santamour 10/20/30 Rule is the global standard for urban forest resilience planning. It states that no single species should exceed 10%, no single genus should exceed 20%, and no single family should exceed 30% of any urban forest. These thresholds protect cities from catastrophic canopy loss when pests, pathogens, or climate events target a single taxonomic group.

The most famous real-world failure of monoculture was the Dutch Elm Disease outbreak of the 1960s–70s, which wiped out hundreds of thousands of elm trees from North American cities that had planted almost nothing else. The same risk applies today with the Emerald Ash Borer and Fraxinus (ash) trees.

Hamilton's urban forest is already in violation at the city level. Acer platanoides (Norway Maple) makes up 11.9% of all trees, exceeding the 10% species cap. The entire Acer genus accounts for 27.1%, far above the 20% genus threshold.
🍁

Species-Level Results: The Norway Maple Problem

At the city level, only one species exceeds the 10% cap: Acer platanoides (Norway Maple) at 11.9%. The next most common species — Sugar Maple (5.0%), Honey Locust (3.9%), Eastern White Cedar (3.6%), Black Walnut (3.6%) — are well below the threshold.

At the ward level, Norway Maple dominates in 12 of 15 wards, reaching a peak of 17.0% in Ward 7 — nearly double the recommended cap. Only Wards 5, 11, and 12 meet the ≤10% species rule.

Genus-Level Results: The Acer Monoculture

The genus Acer (maples) dominates Hamilton's urban forest. At the city level, Acer accounts for 27.1% of all trees — nearly 10 percentage points above the 20% guideline. The next most common genera (Picea at 5.8%, Quercus at 4.5%, Tilia at 4.3%) are far smaller.

At the ward level, only Ward 11 (~19.6%) stays at or below the 20% genus cap. The worst-affected wards are Ward 4 (31.4%), Ward 7 (29.3%), Ward 10 (28.9%), and Ward 3 (28.6%).

Family-Level Results: Sapindaceae Approaching the Limit

At the city level, Sapindaceae (the maple family, which includes all Acer species) accounts for 28.1% of the urban forest — just below the 30% family cap. However, this is the most critical family-level signal: as the Acer genus continues to dominate, Sapindaceae could soon breach the threshold.

No ward currently violates the 30% family rule outright, but Ward 4 is closest at 32.4%.

The family threshold acts as a final safety buffer. Hamilton has not yet crossed it, but the Acer monoculture is pushing Sapindaceae dangerously close. Replanting must introduce species from other families to reduce this pressure.
